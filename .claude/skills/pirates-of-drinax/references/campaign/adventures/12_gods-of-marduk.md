# Gods of Marduk

> Use this when: a fringe anthropologist (**Maris Enar**) hires the crew to crew his research submarine and prove **Marduk**'s "sea god" legends are real — running the fit-out at Marduk Downport, the sea voyage to the **Reinich Archipelago**, the dives over the abyss edge, the sunken-ship breakwater + cliffside temple, first contact with hostile regressed islanders, the squid-god collision, and the climactic storm rescue where the gods reveal themselves as benevolent. A **deniable, low-heat investigation** that builds a relationship with Marduk; no piracy required.

**Logline:** Hired as competent help on a crank-but-real scientist's expedition, the crew transport a submarine to a drowned, regressed island world, dive into the deep to look for "sea gods," uncover an ancient temple guarded by frightened spear-throwing primitives — then a real squid-god rams the sub and a killing storm rolls in, and the only ones who can save the drowning are the very gods they came to prove exist.

## At a glance
- **Location:** **Marduk** (Sindal **0510**, **C577436-5**, Ni Lt, Neutral, FS 1). Starts at **Marduk Downport** (artificial island, large fishing fleet — `key-worlds.md` notes it as GeDeCo-run, though this chapter just calls it "the downport"). Action moves by sea to the **Reinich Archipelago** over the eastern continental-shelf abyss, hundreds of km from the port.
- **Threat/level:** **Phase 1–2, low combat threat.** This is an **investigation + survival** piece, not a fight. The dangers are *environmental* (deep water, a lee-shore storm, drowning) and *social* (a fragile isolated culture you can wipe out by accident). The "monster" sea gods are **never an enemy to be fought** — they have no combat stat block and end up rescuing people. The only stat-blocked opponents are the **Islanders** (and only if the crew start a fight).
- **Est. length:** Short–medium, 1–3 sessions. The fit-out + carousing (Scene 1) is open-endedly expandable; the dives, temple and storm (Scenes 3–7) are the spine. Compressible to one session if the crew push straight to sea.
- **Prerequisites:** None mechanical. The crew need a reason to be at Marduk or passing through — easy to seat, since Marduk is the **jump-2 stepping-stone** between the Torpol Cluster and the Sindalian Main/Thebus. Works as a **patron job** dropped in anywhere (see `00_overview.md`). The crew bring people, not the ship: Maris supplies trawler + submarine.
- **Arc position:** Standalone Companion adventure (no hard links forward). Its payoff is a **goodwill relationship with Marduk** and a momentous discovery (intelligent, benevolent sea gods) the crew must decide whether to publish. Seeds the further-adventure *Marooned on Marduk* (`00_overview.md` "Further Adventures").

## Player-Facing
- **The patron (Maris Enar, anthropologist):** A real, peer-reviewed scientist who operates at the **far crank end** of serious science — the kind who turns up on vid channels with a controversial theory. Funded by **Furtherance**, an investment programme that backs out-there ideas that might pay off. He's prone to chase big what-if questions and miss day-to-day hazards, so he **hires competent people to make his projects work** while he ponders. Loyal to those who impress him; tries to play fair by everyone; **will not knowingly put people at risk for discovery** (but can blunder into danger).
- **The presenting problem / the legend:** Most of Marduk's isolated island enclaves tell tales of **sea gods** — colossal squid-like creatures of the deep: benefactors in some stories, monsters in others, uncaring wonders in a few. Maris has collected many; the similarities convinced him they're real. **No one has ever gone looking**, because the gods supposedly live in very deep water. He wants to prove it.
- **The job (the offer):** Crew his **research submarine**, technicians to tend it, and handy people for the expedition. He's hired a big local **industrial trawler** to carry the sub to a dive site and do a **series of deep dives**. Pay: **Cr5000 each + a small expenses account, 3-week contract.** Core personnel get **bonuses if the trip turns up something saleable**, and his staff are often invited onto **vid shows** to tell their adventures (decent fees; doors open) — a hook for fame-minded crew.
- **Hiring on:** The crew may be hired through a third party and travel to Marduk Downport, or approached as they pass through. The deal is the "simple-yet-interesting" kind.
- **The base ship & its family crew:** the trawler is **mostly automated**, needs only four: master/navigator + engine tech + two deck hands. It's a **family business** — master **Elayn Kirellie**, her husband **Mitchell** and son **Jayce** (deck), and the ship's tech **Eugenie** (Jayce's fiancée). Competent, work well together, baffled by what Maris wants but happy to be paid (the fee beats lost fishing time). The sub seats up to six incl. pilot; carries **tool-arms on its nose, two large + several small ROVs**, and **two deep-diving suits + an airlock** (anyone with Vacc Suit can use a suit; anyone Maris trusts can dive).
- **What the crew can openly perceive:** A genuine but fringe scientist on a real expedition; a stinking fish-trawler retrofitted as a dive ship; an isolated, primitive world; "sea god" superstition (positive and negative) among port locals recruited from the islands. Everything in Scenes 1–4 below is **safe to narrate**.

## GM-Only — Truth & Secrets
- **The sea gods are real, intelligent, and benevolent.** They are colossal squid-like deep-water creatures that **come to the rescue of humans in distress at sea** — exactly as the kindest legends say. Whether they are merely empathic/instinctive or genuinely intelligent (even telepathic/empathic) is left open; Maris suspects **empathy or telepathy** because they're drawn to distressed people in the water and *want to help*. **They are never an enemy.** The mid-adventure "attack" is a **misunderstanding** (below). Keep this gated — the whole emotional arc depends on the crew fearing them first.
- **The temple is a rescue-shrine, not a horror-shrine.** The cliffside carvings (Scene 4) *look* like humans being dragged under by tentacles, but they are the **opposite**: a hopeful depiction of the sea gods as **rescuers** of people in peril at sea — based on an ancient, **true** folk-tale of a ship saved and its crew plucked to safety **on this very island.** The carvings are advanced work (energy-cut, polished stone), hundreds-to-thousands of years old.
- **The sunken-ship "breakwater" — two readings, both partly true.** The ring of freighters east of the archipelago was **deliberately scuttled** (no blast damage; holds intact; filled with building material) to create twin shallow zones that break large waves short of the coast. Reading A: someone defended this holy stretch of coast from storms/tsunami. Reading B (hinted, left open): the ships were sunk to **fence some living thing away from the coast.** In truth this island was a **holy place** of old Marduk; the ships were already there at the fall, and when the **Sindalian bombardment** came, refugees fled here to the temple — a natural sanctuary.
- **The islanders are pitiable, not villains.** They are ~**100 inbred, disorder-ridden** descendants of refugees from the final bombardment, regressed to hunter-gatherer barbarism, with their **own corrupted language** unintelligible to other Marduk dialects. They have a vague concept of "the destruction of the world" and believe their island alone was **saved**. They **worship the temple/sea gods without understanding them** and just want to be left alone. **Not naturally violent** — they try to *scare* intruders off (warning spear-throws, comical chase-displays) and only kill if attacked or pursued. **Their existence is unknown to the rest of the archipelago.** Within 2–3 generations they may die out — a process **trigger-happy crew can finish in an afternoon.** Maris will be **devastated** if any islander is killed, even in self-defence.
- **The "attack" on the sub is a botched rescue.** When the sub jams its nose in the seabed mud (Scene 5), the squid-god that "rammed" it (confused by the sub's **sonar + lights** invading its deep world) **comes back with a second god to free it** — the repeated "collisions" are the gods *bashing it loose from the silt*. Alarming and damaging, but **helping.** Maris pieces this together afterward (Scene 8).
- **The storm is the real climax and the gods' reveal.** Anyone who goes into the water in the storm **would drown** under normal rules — but the sea gods **deposit drowning humans inside the ring of sunken ships**, where the waves carry them ashore near the temple, alive (a brutal, painful, survivable ride). Comrades may believe them dead. **This is the proof Maris came for — delivered by nearly killing everyone.**
- **The deep secret the crew may never learn:** the islanders are the survivors of the bombardment-era refugees; the temple predates the fall by centuries/millennia; the rescue-tale carved there **actually happened**; and the gods "favour the survivors, as they favoured the islanders in their time of need." (Source explicitly flags broader implications as "beyond the scope of this adventure" — good Thread fuel.)

## Scenes / Locations

### 1. Fitting Out — Marduk Downport
**Trigger:** Crew accept Maris's offer at the downport; first job is to ready the trawler for the mission. → **What's here:** Maris has piled up steel, components and cables for a **submarine cradle + winch harness** but can't actually build a working rig. The trawler's family crew *could* try but would be guessing outside their field.
- **The rig: Difficult (10+) Mechanics (INT, 1D hours).** Other crew **join in / build a task chain** (labour, design). Success gets the ship to sea **a few days early** AND **wins the locals' respect** (they admire competence and solving problems with materials at hand). The crew working best-guess take longer and may fail.
- The referee makes as much or as little of fit-out as is fun: **bargaining with maritime-port officials**, evenings in a **fisherfolk bar**, and chats with the trawler crew (a window into local sea-god belief). In theory the conversion + carousing can fill several sessions; move on when the crew want to.
→ **Complications:** none deadly. Use this scene + Scene 2 to **establish relationships** (who likes whom, who seems shifty, who might have an ulterior motive) that color later choices.

### 2. Trials & the Dress Rehearsal — coastal shallows
**Trigger:** Ship ready; Maris spends a couple of days on trials (he's impatient but **not an idiot** — knows this stage matters; may snap, then apologise). → **What's here:** Tedious winch-in/winch-out drills in **shallow, calm** water near the port. The sub seats six; Maris wants everyone who'll work aboard **familiarised with underwater operations** and offers anyone a **dive**. Eugenie is visibly uneasy but goes anyway. Crew can run a series of practice dives, work the **sample-arms/ROVs** (two human-half-size collector ROVs + small camera/light ROVs), and — if they prove competent — earn the right to use the **two deep-diving suits** (Vacc Suit skill to operate; Maris won't hand suits to strangers without a demonstration).
- The trials end with a **full dress rehearsal**: the sub descends into deeper water ringed by its drones, all platforms filming each other — a dramatic "armada of lights." Maris declares the mission **a go.**
→ **Complications:** none mechanical; this is character/atmosphere and a chance to lock in suit/ROV competence before it matters.

### 3. At Sea & the First Dive — Reinich Archipelago
**Trigger:** Three-day voyage to Maris's operational area; weather fine. The trawler reaches the **Reinich Archipelago** (a few mountaintops on the horizon). The seabed **shelves suddenly east of the archipelago** into a deep abyss — Maris's chosen ground (rich nutrient spill over the shelf edge → food → big predators → gods). → **What's here:** A first survey dive maps the seabed and nutrient currents. Winching the sub out **under field conditions** throws up new problems (alarming sways, a creak or two) but nothing major. The ROVs spread wide; data pours in; a seabed map builds. Maris sets the **first deep dive between the two largest sediment spills.**
- The map shows an **anomaly**: a **jagged shallow** amid smooth seabed, near shore but deep enough to investigate. Maris insists. It is **the sunken-ship breakwater** (see GM secret): several big freighters scuttled in **two parallel lines astern**, no blast damage, holds intact, **filled with building material** — a deliberate breakwater (or a fence to pen something away from the coast). The nearest island has no harbour; the trawler **cannot** approach inside the ring; **small inflatables can land.**
- Maris also spots **local fishing boats** in the distance (smaller, primitive; no friction with the port fleet — stocks aren't depleted; they ignore the trawler). He spends an excited evening speculating and **orders a shore expedition** to the nearest island (closest to the deep, "worst hit by a tsunami or storm" — significance he can't yet name).
→ **Complications:** none yet. **Foreshadow the storm** here (the boats will vanish before it hits).

### 4. Exploration Ashore — the cliffside temple & first contact
**Trigger:** Crew take inflatables to the spooky, rocky, forested island. → **Landing: Difficult (10+) Seafarer (personal) (DEX)** to find a safe spot among jagged rocks/surf. → **What's here:** Volcanic rock, thick forest to the waterline, edible fruit, icy streams, jumping arboreal creatures. After a while someone finds a **carved rockface** (looks like a plain outcrop from a distance): humans in peril in the water, giant tentacles rising — some humans grasped by tentacles, others on shore making gestures that read as **worship, terror, or cheering** (deliberately ambiguous). Beautifully done, **energy-cut, weathered, very old.** Shadow-hidden gaps lead into an eerie **inner chamber** open to the sea via an underwater channel (a tidal pool at its centre). Maris is incoherent with excitement — declares it a **temple**, can't say if the gods are good or bad, "scary and powerful." *(GM: it's a rescue-shrine — see secret.)*
- **First contact (the warning):** Amid the surf and Maris's babble, **a lone islander** (skins, stone-tipped spear, feral) enters unseen, freezes, **screeches and bolts.** Moments later **weird yells from all around** and a **spear strikes very close** to a crew member; the thrower (a different islander) steps out and points to **her arm, the spear, the near-missed crew member, and her chest** — i.e. *"Warning shot. The next is lethal."*
→ **Branch (this is the moral hinge of the adventure):**
- **If the crew withdraw / make a show of fleeing (the best outcome):** comical chase-displays, spears thrown deliberately *wide*; the crew get clear. Maris is **delighted** and starts planning a friendly-contact expedition. There is a chance of **amicable contact later** (Scenes 8/Threads).
- **If the crew injure or kill any islander, or pursue:** the islanders **volley spears with lethal intent**, expertly using forest cover. Continued aggression can **massacre the whole ~100-person culture** — which **forecloses the friendly ending**, devastates Maris, and is an **atrocity** (see Empire effects). Spears get rapidly accurate if the crew don't retreat.
→ **Maris's stance:** he **urges the crew not to fight back** and to flee, hoping to return amicably. He'll do all he can to prevent bloodshed and is deeply upset by any islander death.
→ **mythic-gm keyed trigger:** finding the temple is a **must-happen** beat (it "confirms" the gods to Maris and powers the rest). If the crew dawdle ashore, narrate someone stumbling onto the carved face. The **first-contact spear-warning should fire** while they're at the temple — frame it so the crew get a clear *choice* (withdraw vs. escalate); don't rob them of it.

### 5. The Deep Dive — the "attack"
**Trigger:** Whatever happened ashore, Maris is desperate to dive now the gods are "confirmed"; he paces the deck pre-dawn and launches at first light. → **Astute crew warning signs:** the **air smells different, the wind is fresher, dark clouds gather over the deep, and the local fishing boats are gone.** Maris **insists on diving anyway.** → **What's here:** A spectacular descent down the cliff-like shelf edge into darkness (luminous jellyfish, blobby seabed life, ordinary fish — **no god yet**). Ocean ~**2 km deep** here (about half that at the foot of the shelf, then deepening gradually over sediment-fed seabed). The trawler crew grow **uneasy about the weather** (a storm off the open ocean; a **lee shore** behind them; recovering the sub is impossible if the ship rolls heavily). **Elayn urges Maris up; he keeps stalling** for "just another fifteen minutes."
- Then: a disturbance in the silt (Maris briefly thinks it's a god — it's only a current). He noses closer; **navigational sonar goes berserk** — something large and squid-like closes **fast**, possibly a collision course. The sub is **bounced sideways**; only a fleeting glimpse (long body, many tentacles); then gone — and the sub is in trouble.
- **The jam & the "rescue-that-looks-like-an-attack":** the collision drives the **nose into seabed mud**, jamming it; damage alarms light up (hull not leaking — much). **Backing the screw fails** to pull free. The sonar contact returns, flashing close; **a second collision, then another, as giant tentacles grip the sub** — the gods (now **two** of them) are **bashing it loose.** Another impact and it's **free** — screw damaged, propulsion erratic, but ballast allows a **controlled rise.** Going up too fast risks **splitting the hull**; with "several sea gods in the water below apparently intent on bashing the sub to pieces," the crew may risk a fast ascent anyway.
→ **Complications:** keep the gods **terrifying and ambiguous** here — the crew should read it as an attack. There is no to-hit roll for the gods; this is a **narrated set-piece + skill checks** (Engineer/Pilot to manage a damaged, mud-jammed sub; a fast ascent trades hull integrity for escaping the "monsters"). The sub **reaches the surface just as the storm arrives.**
→ **mythic-gm keyed trigger:** the **collision/jam/free sequence is must-happen** (it strands the sub on the surface for the storm). Let crew cleverness ease the *cost* (less hull damage, smoother ascent) but the sub **comes up damaged into a storm** regardless.

### 6. The Storm — the rescue (the deadly climax)
**Trigger:** Sub surfaces into a full storm; the trawler is rolling sickeningly trying to reach it. → **What's here:** The sub **can't survive on the surface** and is **too damaged to dive below the turbulence.** Options, all dangerous: **winch/bring the sub aboard** the rolling trawler (it will **bash the hull, damaging both**; leaks are very unwelcome in these seas); **take off the sub's crew** by small boat (brave, hard); or **run the sub ashore** on the island (passing over the sunken ships in turbulent water — and maybe dealing with the locals again).
- **The trawler crew's code:** they've fought seas like this but **never recovered anything in a storm.** Their instinct is to **save the people and abandon the sub** if needed. **They will not abandon anyone** unless it's hopeless — "you don't leave people to die at sea." If the crew do something **bull-headedly stupid**, the trawler crew **might cut and run… but are more likely to get killed trying to save the idiots.**
- **Throw hazards & hard choices:** crew clinging to safety lines on a heaving deck; the sub hammering the hull; engines/screws failing; gear breaking loose and injuring people. Sample dilemmas: **jury-rig the dead engine or save Elayn from going overboard? Circle for the island's shelter or beach the ship to stop it sinking?** Build appropriate **Seafarer / Engineer (mechanical/electronics) / Athletics / Vacc Suit / Mechanics** checks; this is meant to be a desperate, dramatic gauntlet.
- **Going overboard = drowning… except today.** Anyone whose **safety line breaks** (very likely) ends up in the water — pounded by waves, **certain to drown despite a lifejacket… and then the tentacles.** **The sea gods rescue them:** they **carry the half-drowned survivor inside the ring of sunken ships**, where the waves wash them **ashore near the temple, alive** (dangerous, painful, **survivable**). Comrades may believe them dead.
→ **The locals, again:** anyone washed ashore is treated **very differently** by the islanders — **provided the crew did not massacre them earlier.** The locals approach close enough to be seen, **leave food and water**, and dart back into the forest; **gentle handling can open friendly contact.** *(If the crew slaughtered islanders in Scene 4, expect the opposite.)*
→ **mythic-gm keyed trigger:** **at least one person should end up in the water** (a snapped safety line is "an entirely likely occurrence") so the gods can **reveal their nature by rescuing** them — the adventure's whole point. If the crew play it ultra-safe, let the storm break a line anyway; the rescue is the payoff. **Be honest, though:** a survivor's first experience is *certain death* interrupted by a terrifying tentacle — narrate the genuine danger; the rescue is the twist, not a guarantee the crew can rely on in advance.

### 7. Survive to Morning — Explanations
**Trigger:** The storm is weathered (the trawler may be **driven aground** — a fresh islander encounter; engines/screws may be wrecked; gear may injure people). → **What's here:** In the morning the crew begin to grasp what happened. **Maris has proven the sea gods exist — and they are benevolent.** His (confused, guessing) explanation: the sub's **sonar + lights confused** a god, causing the first collision when it came to investigate the intruder in its world; **far from attacking, it then tried to free the sub from the silt**, and **called a second god to help.** Therefore the gods **communicate with each other**, are **well-disposed to humans**, possibly **intelligent**, and perhaps **telepathic/empathic** (drawn to distressed people, wanting to help) — though he can't say how smart, or **why** they help. He wants a better name for them than "sea gods."
→ **The crew's decision (the adventure's closing question):** Maris starts talking about a **follow-up expedition to live among the islanders** and learn their reading of the temple — "a story for another day." Meanwhile **the crew and Maris must decide whether the world should be told** about the islanders and their gods at all.

**Islanders (regressed hunter-gatherer tribe; the only stat-blocked opponents — and only if attacked)** — Human. **STR 9 DEX 8 END 8 INT 7 EDU 3 SOC 6.** Skills: Athletics (endurance) 1, **Gun Combat (archaic) 1, Melee (blade) 2**, Navigation 0, Recon 1, Stealth 1, Survival 2. ## Rewards & Consequences
- **Pay:** **Cr5000 each** + a small **expenses account**, for the **3-week contract.** **Bonuses** to core personnel **if the expedition turns up something saleable** — and proving intelligent, benevolent sea gods is *extremely* saleable (footage from the sub + ROVs + survivor testimony).
- **Fame / contacts:** Maris's staff are routinely invited onto **vid shows** to tell their adventures (good fees; opens doors) — a tangible reward for fame-seeking crew, and a recurring **patron relationship** with a funded, fair-dealing scientist (and behind him, **Furtherance**, a backer of out-there expeditions → future jobs). The **Kirellie family** become friendly maritime contacts at Marduk if treated well; **Eugenie**'s islander roots are a thread.
- **Knowledge / leverage:** confirmed existence of **intelligent, benevolent deep-sea creatures**; an **ancient energy-cut temple** with true rescue-lore; a hidden **~100-person isolated culture.** The crew **control whether any of this is published** — a genuine choice with real-world consequences for the islanders and the gods (exploitation, tourism, study, or protective silence).
- **Salvage/hardware (minor):** a **research submarine + ROVs + deep-diving suits** and a **trawler** are Maris's, not loot — but goodwill could lend access. Nothing piratical to plunder here.
- **Costs of violence / failure (be honest — no rescues beyond the gods'):**
  - **Massacring the islanders** (Scene 4) **wipes out a unique ~100-person culture**, forecloses the friendly ending, **devastates Maris** (he may end the contract / refuse future work), and is an **atrocity** with reputation fallout (see Empire effects). Wounded islanders **kill with accurate spear-volleys** from cover — a real fight against people who'd rather just scare you off.
  - **Diving into the storm anyway** (Maris insists) strands the **damaged sub on the surface** — the storm is **genuinely lethal**: drowning, the sub bashing the trawler (damaging both; leaks), failing engines, gear injuries, people overboard. **A snapped safety line = drowning** — survivable *only* because the gods intervene, which the crew **cannot count on in advance.**
  - **Bull-headed stupidity** in the rescue can get the **trawler crew killed** trying to save the crew (and may cost the sub, the ship aground, or lives).
